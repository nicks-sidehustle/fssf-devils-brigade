First Special Service Force

They were ordinary men asked to do extraordinary things.

A Plan Born of Desperation

In early 1942, the Allies were losing the war. Nazi Germany controlled most of Europe, and their war machine ran on one crucial resource: hydroelectric power from Norway.

A British journalist and inventor named Geoffrey Pyke proposed something audacious: drop elite commandos by parachute onto Norway's glaciers. From there, they would use special snow vehicles to raid Nazi-controlled power plants, crippling Germany's ability to produce heavy water for atomic research and aluminum for aircraft.

Churchill loved the idea. He called it "Project Plough."

Never in the history of human conflict will so few immobilize so many.
‚Äî Winston Churchill, on Project Plough

The mission required a new kind of soldier ‚Äî men who could ski, parachute, climb mountains, survive arctic conditions, and fight behind enemy lines. America and Canada agreed to form a joint unit ‚Äî the first of its kind ‚Äî to carry out this impossible mission.

The Norway raid was eventually cancelled, but the unit remained. They would find other impossible missions soon enough.

Ordinary Men, Impossible Tasks

They weren't born soldiers. They were lumberjacks and farmers, factory workers and students. Young men from Oklahoma, Montana, Quebec, and Alberta ‚Äî who volunteered when asked to serve.

The Germans would call them "Die Schwarzen Teufel" ‚Äî The Black Devils.

But to themselves, they were just men trying to survive, doing a job they hoped would end the war and get them home.

Training at Fort Harrison

At Fort William Henry Harrison, outside Helena, Montana, volunteers were put through some of the most demanding training of any unit in the war. Not everyone made it through.

They learned to parachute, ski, climb mountains, fight with knives, and demolish targets. They trained in conditions designed to break them ‚Äî because what awaited them would be worse.

The Campaigns

From the Aleutian Islands to the mountains of Italy to the fields of France ‚Äî the Force was sent wherever the mission seemed impossible.

August 15, 1943
Kiska, Alaska
Their first deployment. The Force landed on Kiska Island expecting fierce combat ‚Äî but the Japanese had already evacuated. Despite no enemy contact, 28 Allied servicemen died from friendly fire in the fog and confusion, with 4 more killed by Japanese booby traps left behind. War was already proving unpredictable and costly.

Colonel Robert T. Frederick, his face streaked with camouflage makeup, rests in the grass near Kiska's Quisling Cove, 15 August 1943. Note the FSSF knife insignia on his shoulder.
Source: National Archives

December 3, 1943
Monte la Difensa, Italy
A German stronghold on a 3,000-foot mountain that had stopped every Allied assault. The Force scaled the cliffs at night, in freezing rain, ropes slick with ice. They took the position ‚Äî but at terrible cost. The Force suffered 77% casualties in the Italian mountain campaign.

The Winter Line Campaign, December 1943 - January 1944. Monte la Difensa visible on the left.
Source: "The Supercommandos" by Robert Todd Ross

February - May 1944
Anzio Beachhead
99 days of continuous combat. Just 1,300 Forcemen held a 13-kilometer front ‚Äî a sector that would normally require a 10,000-man division. They compensated with aggressive night raids, blackening their faces and slipping into German lines. They left calling cards on enemy bodies: their unit patch with the warning "Das dicke Ende kommt noch!" (The worst is yet to come).

Liberation of Rome
The Force was among the first Allied units to enter Rome ‚Äî the first Axis capital to fall. Just two days later, the world's attention shifted to D-Day. But the men who fought to Rome knew what it cost.

December 5, 1944
Disbandment
After Rome and Southern France, the Force was officially disbanded near Villeneuve-Loubet, France. The unit never failed a mission ‚Äî disbandment came because Canada could no longer replace its casualties. The Americans and Canadians who had fought as brothers said goodbye ‚Äî many for the last time. But for many, the war wasn't over. American Forcemen were reassigned to the 474th Infantry Regiment or various airborne divisions. In a twist of fate, the 474th later served occupation duty in Norway ‚Äî ironically fulfilling the Force's original intended destination. Roland continued serving, eventually earning a Presidential Unit Citation with the 501st Parachute Infantry Regiment.

What People Read at Home

While the soldiers fought, families back home followed the war through newspapers and radio. Here's how the news reported on the campaigns where the Force served.

‚òÖ THE STARS AND STRIPES ‚òÖ

Mountain Stronghold Falls
to Night Assault

ITALIAN FRONT, Dec. 4 ‚Äî A German fortress position atop Monte la Difensa, which had repulsed repeated Allied attacks over three weeks, fell early this morning to a surprise assault by American and Canadian special forces.

The assault troops scaled a 1,000-foot cliff in darkness and freezing rain, achieving complete tactical surprise. The position was secured within two hours of the initial attack.

German prisoners expressed disbelief that any force could have climbed the sheer rock face they had considered impassable. Allied commanders praised the unit's training and determination.

Rome Falls to Allies;
First Axis Capital Liberated

ROME, June 5 ‚Äî American forces entered the Eternal City yesterday, liberating the first capital of an Axis power to fall to Allied arms.

Joyful Romans filled the streets to greet their liberators, throwing flowers and offering wine to the exhausted but triumphant soldiers.

The capture follows months of bitter fighting from the Anzio beachhead and through the heavily defended Gustav Line. German forces retreated northward as Allied units pushed into the city from multiple directions.

The Cost

The numbers tell part of the story. But numbers can't capture what these men carried with them for the rest of their lives.

1,800 Combat Troops
600%+ Casualty Turnover
2,314 Total Casualties
251 Days of Combat

The casualty rate of over 600% means the unit was essentially replaced more than six times over.

Coming Home

When the war ended, the surviving members of the Force came home to a world that had moved on without them. The hardest battles were often yet to come.

The Silence

Most veterans never talked about what they saw. There was no word for PTSD then ‚Äî they called it "combat fatigue" or "shell shock," and men were expected to just get over it. Many never did. They carried the war quietly, alone.

The Nightmares

Night raids meant killing in the dark, up close and personal. Those memories don't fade. Many veterans struggled with nightmares for decades. Some turned to alcohol. Some couldn't hold jobs or maintain relationships.

The Missing Faces

Every survivor carried the weight of friends who didn't make it. In a unit with 600% casualty turnover, everyone lost someone. That kind of loss shapes a person forever.

Rebuilding

Despite everything, most tried to build normal lives. They married, raised families, worked jobs. Some found peace in the mountains and forests that reminded them of Montana training. Others couldn't escape the war no matter how hard they tried.

We weren't heroes. We were just young men doing a terrible job that had to be done. The real heroes are the ones who didn't come back.
‚Äî FSSF Veteran

Roland's Story

One of those young men was Roland Oroville Morgan ‚Äî a soldier in the 4th Company, 1st Regiment of the First Special Service Force.

Roland enlisted on July 13, 1940 ‚Äî more than a year before Pearl Harbor ‚Äî and served until July 29, 1945. A young man from Oklahoma who answered his country's call before most Americans knew war was coming ‚Äî and left behind someone he loved.

Roland didn't just serve. He fought. He bled. He survived.

After the war, Roland came home to Georgiana. They married and settled in Montana ‚Äî Georgiana's hometown, and near Fort Harrison where he'd trained with the Force.

Together they raised six children, including Jeannette, Dean's grandma. Roland's story didn't end in the war. It continued in the family he built.

Legacy

In 2015, surviving members of the First Special Service Force ‚Äî most in their 90s ‚Äî received the Congressional Gold Medal, the highest civilian honor in the United States. For many, it came 70 years late. For some, it came after they had already passed.

The Force is recognized as the ancestor of modern Special Forces ‚Äî the Green Berets, the Canadian Special Operations Regiment. But the men who served weren't thinking about legacy. They were thinking about survival, and about getting home.

Remember

The men of the First Special Service Force were not superheroes. They were human beings ‚Äî scared, exhausted, far from home ‚Äî who did what was asked of them at terrible cost.

They didn't see themselves as heroes. They just wanted to survive ‚Äî and be remembered.

This is for Roland Morgan, and for all the men of the Force ‚Äî the ones who came home, and the ones who didn't.
