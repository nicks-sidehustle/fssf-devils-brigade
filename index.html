Training at Fort Harrison

At Fort William Henry Harrison in Helena, Montana, volunteers were put through some of the most demanding training of any unit in the war. Not everyone made it through.

They learned to parachute, ski, climb mountains, fight with knives, and demolish targets. They trained in conditions designed to break them â€” because what awaited them would be worse.

The Cost

The numbers tell part of the story. But numbers can't capture what these men carried with them for the rest of their lives.

2,314 Original Members
600%+ Casualty Turnover
434 Killed in Action
1,800+ Wounded

The casualty rate of over 600% means the unit was essentially replaced more than six times over. Most of the men who started were not there at the end.

Coming Home

When the war ended, the surviving members of the Force came home to a world that had moved on without them. The hardest battles were often yet to come.

The Silence

Most veterans never talked about what they saw. There was no word for PTSD then â€” they called it "combat fatigue" or "shell shock," and men were expected to just get over it. Many never did. They carried the war quietly, alone.

The Nightmares

Night raids meant killing in the dark, up close and personal. Those memories don't fade. Many veterans struggled with nightmares for decades. Some turned to alcohol. Some couldn't hold jobs or maintain relationships.

The Missing Faces

Every survivor carried the weight of friends who didn't make it. In a unit with 600% casualty turnover, everyone lost someone. That kind of loss shapes a person forever.

Rebuilding

Despite everything, most tried to build normal lives. They married, raised families, worked jobs. Some found peace in the mountains and forests that reminded them of Montana training. Others couldn't escape the war no matter how hard they tried.
